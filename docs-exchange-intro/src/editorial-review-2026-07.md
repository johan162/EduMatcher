# Editorial Review: "How a Financial Exchange Works"

Review date: 2026-07-06. Scope: all chapters in `00-frontmatter` through `04-technology-and-infrastructure`, plus glossary and references. Organized per the five review areas requested.

---

## 1. Incorrect or questionable statements

**Duplicated paragraph.** In `01-foundation/05-a-brief-tour-of-real-world-exchanges.md`, the entire "Nasdaq Stockholm" section (three paragraphs, from "Nasdaq Stockholm is Sweden's primary regulated..." through "...operating inside a larger cross-border technology and regulatory framework") is printed twice, back to back, verbatim. This is a copy-paste artifact and should be deduplicated.

**"SBR" vs "SBE."** In `03-risk-and-compliance/03-self-match-prevention...md`, the section header asks "What is the SBE?" but the body text answers "**SBR** stands for *Simple Binary Encoding (SBE)*..." SBR is not a real expansion of the acronym; every other reference in the book (correctly) calls it SBE. Simple typo, but a confusing one directly under a heading meant to define the term.

**The SpaceX / "blue chip" comparison doesn't hold together.** In `01-foundation/01-before-the-exchange...md`, the account of SpaceX's 2026 NASDAQ IPO is broadly accurate (it priced at $135, opened around $150, and closed day one near $161, implying a market cap in the $2.1–2.2 trillion range) — the specific "$2.01 trillion closing" figure is a little off versus reporting (closer to $2.2T after the ~19% first-day gain), worth a quick number check against the final S-1/exchange print. More importantly, the surrounding claim that the $135 opening price was "not a blue chip price" because blue chip status "traditionally means an evaluation of ≥$200 per share" is a non-sequitur. The $200 figure comes from Oliver Gingold's 1923 usage (cited correctly elsewhere in the book), a nominal dollar amount from over a century ago with no inflation adjustment, no accounting for stock splits, and no relationship to how "blue chip" is actually used today (which is about company quality and durability, not a share-price threshold at all — Berkshire Hathaway trades near $700,000/share and Apple near $200 after multiple splits; neither fact bears on "blue chip" status). This paragraph should either be cut or reframed as a historical curiosity rather than a comparison that reads as if it means something analytically.

**Japan Exchange Group ranking.** `01-foundation/05-a-brief-tour...md` and elsewhere describe JPX as "the world's third-largest exchange by market capitalisation of listed companies, behind NYSE and NASDAQ." Current data is ambiguous on this: depending on the year and whether Shanghai and Shenzhen are counted as one market or two, Shanghai Stock Exchange's listed market cap has recently exceeded JPX's, which would make JPX fourth, not third. Recommend either sourcing a specific dated ranking (e.g., "as of [month/year], per WFE statistics") or softening to "among the world's largest."

**Grokipedia as a citation source.** Two footnotes lean on grokipedia.com (an AI-generated wiki) for historical claims: Jonathan's Coffee House (ref. inline link in `01-foundation/02-...md`) and the Mizuho fat-finger incident ([16] in the references). The underlying facts check out against Wikipedia and press coverage, but an AI-generated wiki is not an appropriate citation for a book that otherwise cites Petram, Michie, Harris, and primary regulatory reports. Recommend re-pointing these two citations to the Wikipedia articles already listed alongside them, or to a citable news source, and dropping Grokipedia from the reference list entirely — its presence undercuts the credibility of an otherwise well-sourced text.

**Regulation NMS date given two ways.** `01-foundation/02-what-is-a-financial-exchange...md` says Reg NMS is from "2005," while `04-technology.../06-smart-order-routing...md` says it was "implemented in 2007." Both are defensible (adopted 2005, phased compliance through 2007) but stated flatly as if there's one date. A one-line reconciliation ("adopted in 2005, phased in through 2007") would remove the apparent contradiction.

**Minor internal inconsistency on IEX's founding.** The "Brief Tour" chapter says IEX "launched as a dark pool in 2013," while the Speed Bumps chapter says IEX was "founded in 2012." These aren't necessarily contradictory (company founded 2012, dark-pool operations began 2013) but as written they read as two different founding years. Worth a clarifying word ("founded in 2012, began operating as a dark pool in 2013").

**A citation typo:** reference [14] attributes the Oliver Gingold "blue chip" sourcing to "Philadelphis University" (should be Philadelphia).

None of the core mechanics content — order types, price-time priority, matching logic, auctions, clearing, market data — contains factual errors as far as this review found. The regulatory dates, historical events (Black Monday, the 2010 Flash Crash, Knight Capital, Barings/Leeson, Lehman, Herstatt, the 2020 COVID halts), and named products (ITCH/OUCH, MDP3/SBE, FIX) all check out against public sources.

## 2. Numerical examples

This is good news: every worked numerical example in the book was re-derived independently and all of them are internally consistent — the dilution example (80%/$1M, 20%/$250k), the market-cap arithmetic ($2.9T), the VWAP sweep ($150.395), the pro-rata allocation (43/17 lots), the full implied-order walkthrough (January/February/spread legs), the midpoint-peg savings ($5,000/$5,000), the equilibrium-auction table ($151, 1,500 shares), the index divisor worked example ($7.0071B divisor, ~1045 after Alpha's move), and the P&L/VWAP/margin examples in the clearing chapter (VWAP $153.33, unrealised P&L $1,000.50, variation margin $50,000) all check out arithmetically. The only numeric item worth a second look is the SpaceX closing-price figure noted above, and the unsourced "CME Globex handles approximately 30–35 million messages per day" claim — this is plausible for trade/contract volume but exchange *message* counts (which include quotes and cancels, not just fills) for a venue at CME's scale are typically much larger; recommend citing a specific, dated CME statistic rather than a round unattributed figure.

## 3. Convoluted or missing explanations

The trailing-stop, midpoint-peg, and implied-order sections are genuinely well done — each anticipates the reader's confusion and resolves it with a concrete worked example, which is exactly the right pattern for this material.

The weakest explanation in the book is the treatment of **tick size** itself (currently a few paragraphs inside "Price-Time Priority, The Fairness Rule" in Part II). It asserts that ticks are stored as integers and that non-aligned prices are rejected, but doesn't explain: how a midpoint price is rounded when the sum of bid+ask lands on a half-tick (the "Order Types" chapter mentions "requiring care with rounding to the nearest valid tick" for midpoint pegs but never shows the rounding rule or a worked example of what breaks if it's done inconsistently); how tick size regimes vary by price band (e.g., sub-$1 stocks often get sub-penny ticks, or venues with banded tick schedules like the LSE); or how markets operated before decimalization, when US equities traded in eighths and sixteenths of a dollar. Given this is flagged below as a missing chapter, folding the existing tick material into that new chapter (rather than leaving it as a subsection under Price-Time Priority) would also fix the current awkward organization, where a foundational concept used throughout the book is introduced almost in passing, two-thirds of the way into Part II.

Two smaller clarity gaps: the "Odd lot" concept (orders below the standard round lot size) is never defined despite lot size being introduced in "The Order" chapter — worth a short explanation, since odd lots have historically been excluded from NBBO calculations in US equities, a detail that surprises many developers. And "locked" and "crossed" markets (where the NBBO bid and ask briefly invert across venues due to propagation latency) are a natural companion to the NBBO/fragmentation discussion in Part IV but are never mentioned, even though they're a common source of "this looks like a bug but isn't" confusion for anyone building against real market data.

## 4. Missing major chapters

In rough order of priority:

**Tick Size and Fractional Ticks (Part II).** As requested — this deserves to be promoted from a subsection to its own chapter. It should cover: why prices are stored as integer tick counts; how tick size interacts with price bands (variable tick regimes, e.g., larger ticks for higher-priced stocks on some venues); the SEC's Tick Size Pilot Program (2016–2018) for small-cap stocks and what it revealed; the history of US decimalization (equities traded in fractions — eighths, then sixteenths — until April 2001, when the SEC mandated the move to decimals, a change that materially widened effective tick granularity and remains one of the most consequential market-structure changes in modern history); sub-penny quoting restrictions (Reg NMS Rule 612, which bans quoting most stocks above $1 in increments finer than a cent); and a worked numerical example of a rounding bug (e.g., a midpoint calculation landing on a half-cent, and the consequences of rounding it inconsistently between two components of the same system).

**Trade Busting and Clearly Erroneous Trades.** Part III covers prevention (fat-finger filters, collars, kill switches) thoroughly but never covers what happens *after* a bad trade has already executed. Exchanges have formal, rules-based processes for busting or price-adjusting "clearly erroneous" trades after the fact (with specific percentage thresholds and timing windows), and this is a distinct mechanism from any pre-trade control. It's also directly relevant to two events already in the book (the 2010 Flash Crash's "$0.01 or $100,000" trades and the 2012 Knight Capital trades were both subject to after-the-fact busting/adjustment decisions), so it would tie the existing narrative together rather than requiring new material to be introduced from scratch.

**Options Mechanics: Exercise, Assignment, and Expiry.** Options are referenced throughout (OCC, Cboe, straddles/strangles, the VIX) but there's no chapter on what actually happens at expiry — exercise, assignment, automatic exercise thresholds, and how this differs operationally from futures settlement (already covered). Given how often options vocabulary appears elsewhere, a short chapter would pay for itself.

**Listing and Delisting Mechanics.** The book covers the IPO from the corporate-finance side (Part I) and corporate actions like delisting-after-acquisition (Part IV), but never covers the exchange's own listing standards and process: initial listing requirements (minimum share price, market cap, shareholder count), continued-listing compliance and cure periods, direct listings as an alternative to a traditional underwritten IPO, and SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company) mergers as a third path to public markets — a major feature of the 2020–2021 market that would fit naturally alongside the existing IPO material.

**Cryptocurrency and Digital Asset Venues.** For a book being written in 2026, the complete absence of crypto exchanges is conspicuous. Even a short chapter contrasting 24/7 continuous trading (no session state machine), on-chain settlement replacing T+1 CCP clearing, and the absence of a single regulator equivalent to the SEC would situate the rest of the book's assumptions (which are all built around traditional, regulated, session-based markets) as a specific model rather than the only model.

**Market Data Economics.** Part IV covers market data *architecture* in depth (ITCH, snapshots, conflation) but not market data as a *business* — the SIP vs. proprietary-feed controversy, exchange market-data revenue as a growing and contested share of exchange profits, and the ongoing SEC market-data-infrastructure rulemaking. This connects naturally to the existing PFOF and best-execution material in the Smart Order Routing chapter.

**Conformance Testing and Onboarding.** A short but practically important gap: how a new participant actually connects to an exchange for the first time (certification/conformance testing environments, UAT, FIX certification), which is completely different in character from the production risk-control material in Part III.

## 5. Historical reflections to add

The book already has strong historical grounding (VOC/Amsterdam 1602, Le Maire 1609, Black Monday 1987, the 2010 Flash Crash, Knight Capital 2012, Barings 1995, Lehman 2008, Herstatt 1974, Mizuho 2005, Sarao spoofing, Peterffy vs. NASDAQ 1983). The following are events of comparable significance that are currently absent, ordered by how directly they'd strengthen chapters that already exist:

**May Day, 1 May 1975.** The SEC abolished fixed brokerage commissions on this date, ending nearly 200 years of fixed-rate trading dating back to the 1792 Buttonwood Agreement (which the book already covers). This single regulatory change created the discount-brokerage industry and is arguably the direct ancestor of the "retail flow is profitable" and PFOF dynamics the book discusses at length in Part IV — it belongs either in Part I alongside the Buttonwood Agreement or as context for the PFOF section. (SEC historical release; Gregoriou & Skerratt, "The London Big Bang" for the UK parallel below.)

**The Facebook IPO glitch, NASDAQ, 18 May 2012.** A matching-engine software defect in NASDAQ's own IPO cross process delayed Facebook's opening trade by roughly 30 minutes and caused widespread order-confirmation failures; NASDAQ paid an SEC-record (at the time) $10 million fine and separately compensated market makers roughly $62 million for losses. This is the natural counterpart to Knight Capital: Knight shows a *participant*-side deployment failure, Facebook/NASDAQ shows an *exchange*-side one, and it belongs in Part III or as a companion cautionary tale right next to Knight Capital. (SEC Release No. 70694, May 2013.)

**BATS's own botched IPO, 23 March 2012.** BATS Global Markets attempted to list its own shares on its own exchange; a software bug in the BATS matching engine itself caused BATS stock to crash from $15.25 to a fraction of a cent within about 900 milliseconds of the open, forcing BATS to withdraw its own IPO minutes later. Rarely does a single event so neatly combine "test your matching engine" with "your own IPO is not exempt from your own bugs" — an excellent, short, and slightly ironic addition to Part III or Part IV.

**The 2021 GameStop / Robinhood episode.** In late January 2021, retail-driven demand for GameStop (and other heavily shorted names) caused the DTCC's National Securities Clearing Corporation to issue an extraordinary intraday increase in clearing-fund deposit requirements to Robinhood (reportedly a ten-fold increase overnight), which Robinhood could not immediately meet in full, forcing it to temporarily restrict buying in the stock. This is arguably the most consequential real-world illustration of the clearing/margin chapter's core claim ("the CCP's protection isn't free — someone has to fund it in real time") and directly touches PFOF, retail order flow, and short-selling/borrow mechanics, all of which are already covered elsewhere in the book. (House Financial Services Committee report, June 2021; DTCC public statements, January 2021.)

**China's circuit-breaker withdrawal, January 2016.** China introduced a market-wide circuit-breaker system on 1 January 2016 modelled partly on the US approach, but miscalibrated thresholds (a 7% market-wide halt that, once triggered, was followed almost immediately by a second, deeper halt on the same day) caused panic-buying-ahead-of-the-halt behaviour that made volatility worse, not better. China suspended the mechanism after only four trading days. This is a valuable counter-example to sit next to the book's otherwise uniformly positive circuit-breaker narrative — it shows that badly calibrated halts can actively destabilize a market, which is exactly the kind of design trade-off the "Should Halt Duration Be Fixed or Proportional" section already raises in the abstract.

**The London "Big Bang," 27 October 1986.** The LSE's deregulation abolished fixed commissions, allowed foreign and non-member ownership of brokerage firms, and replaced open-outcry floor trading with the screen-based SEAQ system, all in a single day. It's the UK's direct parallel to both May Day (1975) and the book's NASDAQ-vs-floor narrative, and belongs in Part I next to the existing LSE and coffee-house material.

**US equity decimalization, completed April 2001.** Already noted above as essential context for a new tick-size chapter, this belongs equally on the historical-reflections list: US markets traded in fractions of a dollar (eighths, then sixteenths) from their founding until this date, and the change to decimals is one of the most-studied market-structure transitions in the academic literature (it's directly connected to the [1] Harris and [2] Hasbrouck references already in the bibliography).
